THE INDIAN RIVER SYSTEM

India is a land of rivers, and its river system plays a vital role in shaping the geography, culture, agriculture, and economy of the country.

The Indian river system comprises a vast network of major and minor rivers that flow across the length and breadth of the subcontinent. These rivers originate from the Himalayan mountains, central highlands, or Western Ghats and flow into the Bay of Bengal, the Arabian Sea, or inland basins.

The Indian rivers are broadly classified into two main categories: Himalayan rivers and Peninsular rivers. The Himalayan rivers, such as the Ganga, Yamuna, and Brahmaputra, are perennial, meaning they flow throughout the year, fed by both rainfall and melting glaciers.

In contrast, the Peninsular rivers like the Godavari, Krishna, and Cauvery are mostly rain-fed and may become seasonal. Rivers in India not only support agriculture through irrigation but also provide water for domestic and industrial use, generate hydroelectric power, and serve as important means of transportation in certain regions. Culturally and spiritually, rivers are considered sacred and hold a significant place in Indian traditions and rituals.

GANGA RIVER SYSTEM

1. Introduction

The Ganga River System is the largest and most significant river system in India. It is not only vital from a geographical and ecological perspective but also holds immense religious, cultural, and historical importance for millions of Indians. The Ganga is considered sacred in Hinduism and is often referred to as "Ganga Maa" (Mother Ganga).

2. Origin The Ganga originates as the Bhagirathi River from the Gangotri Glacier in the Himalayas, located in Uttarakhand. It becomes the Ganga River officially at Devprayag, where the Bhagirathi and Alaknanda rivers merge.

3. Major Tributaries The Ganga River System includes numerous tributaries from both the Himalayas and the Peninsular plateau. These are classified into:

4. Course of the Ganga

Uttarakhand: Originates from Gangotri glacier as Bhagirathi. Uttar Pradesh: Flows through important cities like Haridwar, Kanpur, and Allahabad (Prayagraj). Bihar: Joined by major tributaries like Kosi and Gandak. West Bengal: Enters as the Padma River into Bangladesh after splitting near Farakka. Total Length: Approximately 2,525 kilometers Drainage Area: About 10 lakh sq. km (1,000,000 sq km) States Covered: Uttarakhand, Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Jharkhand, West Bengal Countries Covered: India and Bangladesh

5. Mouth and Delta

The Ganga, after entering Bangladesh, merges with the Brahmaputra and flows into the Bay of Bengal as the Meghna River. It forms the Sundarbans Delta, the largest delta in the world, which is also home to the famous Royal Bengal Tiger.

6. Importance

Agriculture: Supports extensive irrigation in the fertile Indo-Gangetic Plains. Hydropower: Dams and barrages provide hydroelectric power. Transport: Navigable in certain stretches. Religion and Culture: Sacred river, with major pilgrimage sites such as Varanasi, Haridwar, and Prayagraj. Ecology: Supports diverse flora and fauna.

7. Challenges Pollution: Due to sewage, industrial waste, and religious offerings. Overuse: Excessive irrigation and water diversion. Climate change: Impact on glacier sources. Urbanization: Encroachment on floodplains and riverbanks.

8. Conservation Efforts Namami Gange Programme: Launched by the Government of India to clean and rejuvenate the Ganga.
